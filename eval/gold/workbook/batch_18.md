Baldur's Gate 3 isn’t just a great RPG—it’s the kind of game that makes most other RPGs feel slightly unfinished afterward.

At its core, it’s a **turn-based, party-based CRPG set in the Dungeons & Dragons universe**, where almost everything is driven by choice: combat, dialogue, exploration, and even failure. The game constantly reacts to what you do in ways that feel genuinely handcrafted rather than scripted. That level of reactivity is a big reason it’s been called one of the best RPGs ever made.

The biggest strength is how much **player freedom and agency** it gives you. You can solve problems through combat, stealth, persuasion, absurd physics, or just creative nonsense the devs somehow accounted for. It feels less like “following a questline” and more like *actually playing a tabletop campaign with a very ambitious DM*.

Combat is turn-based but extremely flexible. Positioning, abilities, environmental interactions, and party synergy all matter, and even fights you “mess up” can turn into memorable chaos instead of just a reload moment.

And then there’s the writing and characters. The companions are a huge highlight—well-voiced, deeply written, and reactive to your decisions in ways that actually change relationships and outcomes. It’s one of those rare games where you end up caring about your party way more than expected.

But it’s not flawless.

The biggest barrier for some players is the **sheer complexity and pacing**. The game is dense. Systems stack on top of systems (spells, status effects, gear interactions, dialogue checks), and it can feel overwhelming if you’re not used to CRPGs or D&D-style rules. Even fans admit it can feel like “homework” managing everything at times.

There’s also the reality that the game is so big that you can easily miss entire storylines or regions without realizing it. That freedom is impressive, but it can also mean uneven pacing depending on how you play.

And while later acts are still strong, some players note occasional performance dips or rougher pacing compared to the near-perfect opening.

Still, none of that really changes the overall picture.

With **overwhelmingly positive Steam reviews (around 96%+ positive across hundreds of thousands of players)**, it’s clear how widely it’s loved and how impactful it’s been.

**Pros:**

* Insane player freedom and reactivity
* Deep, tactical combat with endless creativity
* Incredible writing and memorable companions
* Huge amount of content and replayability
* Choices actually matter in meaningful ways

**Cons:**

* Very steep learning curve (especially for newcomers)
* Can feel overwhelming or slow to manage
* Easy to miss content due to branching structure
* Some pacing/performance issues in later sections

**Verdict:**
A landmark RPG that sets a new standard for choice-driven storytelling and reactive game design. It’s not always simple, but it’s almost always impressive.

**10/10**
