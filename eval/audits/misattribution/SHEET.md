## 031 · Baldur's Gate 3 — **learning_curve** (pinned), sentiment **negative**

review `224424392` · mention `93735`

> For decades I’ve played many RPG type games but none that resemble a D&D experience so I’m definitely a newbie in that category so decided to give it a go. 
> 
> There are many reviews that cover a lot of aspects of this game and I noticed that they all tend to be from those who are familiar D&D; as I’m not I can’t really relate to how much praise this game was getting so if your new this sort of thing as well this review is for you. 
> 
> I’m not going to delve into the story as that will move into spoiler territory; what I will say is the game has an interesting story with subplots that spans three main acts, each act has a varied environment with a huge amount to explore (if your inclined to look about).
> I would say each act is large enough to be a complete game in its own right and as a newbie the first act alone took me 60 hours due to reloads and having to go to other sources to understand game mechanics (more on that later).
> 
> When the game begins, you’ll start by selecting a character’s race and traits, these are selectable via some built in characters, there’s also the option to manually create your own (the latter will appeal to those familiar with D&D or who want to roll a custom character for subsequent playthroughs).
> I was not familiar with that to pick so I went for a predefined main character for my play through. 
> 
> As you work your way through each act, you’ll meet a lot of entities, most of these characters can be spoken to (including animals and the dead) though not all have conversation branches, though surprisingly a lot of them do.  
> Some conversations can have relevance to either quest clues or open new quests entirely impacting overall game outcomes in surprising ways.
> Some of the characters might like to accompany you on your adventures (you can have a max of 4 in a party) or you can ask them stay at your camp so you can swap party members as needed.
> Characters skills and magic traits are varied so keeping some on hand can have strategic advantages depending on any given situation, it’s a personal choice there no hard rule as to what you should do. 
> 
> Sound and graphics 
> The Game is a modern title and graphics reflect that, they're highly stylised for the biomes which are considerably different in each act and the use of effects, such as magic casting fits perfectly into those biomes.
> The music and sound effects are just as impressive for both the environment and combat.  
> 
> To move about, a floating 3D camera is operated (with zoom) showing the 3d world from a third person view.
> To move the characters, each can be selected and a location clicked with the mouse, you can select between group move or individuals, the latter can be important for combat preparation before engaging. 
>  
> 
> Game Learning Curve. 
> The game says it uses Dungeons & Dragons 5th Edition (D&D 5e) rulebook, which is great for people who know D&D but meant nothing to me, but in summary it determines the rules to what can and can’t be done in game. 
> So to get the best out of this game, it is necessary to understand a lot of the game mechanics to how (and why) things work as they do, for the newbie this was by far the most confusing aspect of the game. 
> 
> Combat scenarios 
> You’ll come across all manner of enemies in game, most with specific skills and combat + magic moves that need to be analysed to determine how to defeat them.  I liked this aspect of the game as it required me to think about how to attack and defend, it’s not fast action or easy (in most cases) but all combat was satisfying when foes got defeated.
> 
> The game is a turn-based system and during combat, each of your team members and enemy have a turn to do something, this is where a lot of the confusion came from early in the game due to not understand those pesky D&D rules I spoke about earlier.
> For example, I might want to move myself closer to an enemy to attack only to find I could move but was not allowed to perform an actual attack till my next move. This led to some very frustrating game play as I didn’t understand why certain moves\actions etc, would work in a single turn and others wouldn’t.  
> 
> The game does have some very basic tutorials, it also contains decent descriptions of what each action and spell can do including some stat such as what you’ll gain (or lose) using said spell, action, bonus action etc. 
> The issue here is that it doesn’t read well if you’ve never played this sort of game.
> Normally in those game situations I use trial and error which worked at the beginning of the game but that didn’t prepare me for the more complex battles, I just didn’t understand why things worked the way they did and this led me to a massive disadvantage resulting to going back to the load screen frequently.
> 
> The way round this is to use your favourite Chat system with a phrase like: “Latest version of BDG3, no spoilers explain how Dash works for a move, or what a Spell slot is, or what does an intelligence Save mean” and so on.
> The chat system does a decent job of explain the terminology and honestly saved me from giving up on this game entirely. 
> Understanding how the systems works really opens up the game and allows you to see what your enemy’s are doing (and why) this can change the tide of a battle immensely. 
> The same can be said when correctly reading the stats and equipment of my own character and my companions  and completely opens the game up to a whole new level making it a far more enjoyable experience. 
> The good news is that it’s not that difficult to understand and be assured that the  more complex stuff is being handled in the background by the game engine so all you see is a  pop up message with a result that makes sense.
> 
> 
> Game Variety 
> One of the praises the game gets from other reviews is just how varied things are during exploration and combat situations and there not wrong. 
> When navigating environments, you might want to try different techniques to give you the upper edge. 
> For example, in combat got a few solders running to strike you, spill a grease bottle on the floor (can make enemies slip) then set fire to a grease spillage burning those in it and a lot more.
> Come across a door you don’t have the key for, try lock picking or what about knocking it down with a sword and so on.
>  
> 
> In Summary 
> + A extremely varied (and long) game with many characters and environments to interact with
> + Decisions you make greatly impact how quests unfold with changes to yourself, and others 
> + Good use of graphics and sound 
> + User interface\ Inventory is well laid out (once understood)   
> + A good save and load system
> + Lots of ways and techniques in which to tackle the game 
> 
> - ⟦Learning curve for players new to D&D will be steep and its not very well explained in game⟧. 
> Tip: Use chat tools to explain things. the game behaviour and rules are not actually that difficult to understand once you get a context and it will improve your gaming experience immensely.
> - The whole game is a little too long and pacing can be a little slow in places. 
> - Some quest markers are not very clear; there either in the wrong place or quests can't be completed until you move to a new Act.  You get a warning with the latter but its not a clear what quests might need to completed where you are or what quests need to be followed up when you move to on. 
> - Inventory system is fine, but the Camp chest (the large inventory) has no search option in it.
> - There are a few bugs, (not many) though it needed looking up outside the game to determine if what was happening (or not happening) was part of the game experience or a known bug.
